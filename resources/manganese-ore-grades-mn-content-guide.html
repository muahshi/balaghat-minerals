Manganese Ore Grades Explained: Mn% 30-32, 38-40, 44-46, 46-48

Buyer's Guide · Balaghat Minerals

If you're sourcing manganese ore for a steel mill, ferro-alloy plant, or trading desk, the single most important number on any spec sheet is the Mn% (manganese content). It determines pricing, freight economics, and whether the ore meets your furnace's metallurgical requirements. This guide breaks down the common grade bands and what they mean in practice.

Why Mn% Drives Everything

Manganese ore is priced largely on a per-unit-of-Mn basis once you account for impurities like iron (Fe), silica (SiO2), and phosphorus. A higher Mn% ore delivers more usable manganese per tonne shipped, which matters when freight is a large share of landed cost — especially for buyers in West Bengal, Gujarat, or overseas markets paying CIF.

Other Specs That Matter Alongside Mn%

  • Fe (Iron): Affects the Mn:Fe ratio in the furnace charge — too much Fe can dilute the alloy you're targeting.
  • SiO2 (Silica): Higher silica increases slag volume and energy consumption per tonne of alloy produced.
  • Phosphorus: Critical for buyers producing low-phosphorus alloy grades; even small variances can affect acceptance.
  • Size (mm): Lump sizing (commonly 5–100mm or 25–80mm) affects furnace charging and screening requirements at the buyer's plant.
  • Moisture: Typically capped at 1–5% — relevant for weight-based pricing and furnace stability.

How Grade Affects Pricing Structure

Export and domestic purchase orders for manganese ore commonly specify a base Mn% with a "guarantee" clause — if the delivered material comes in below the agreed Mn%, the buyer applies a pro-rata price deduction based on the analysis report from their own plant lab. This is standard practice and protects both sides: the seller is paid fairly for what's actually delivered, and the buyer doesn't overpay for under-spec material.
